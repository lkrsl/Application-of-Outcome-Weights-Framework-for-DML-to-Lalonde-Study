Chapter 4 LaLonde (NSW) Data

This section (4) examines the effect of the treatment (participation in the job training program) on the participants’ earnings in 1978 (re78) in the original LaLonde dataset (loaded as nsw, similar to Imbens & Xu).

4.3.2 Weighting

4.3.2.1 Inverse probability weights (IPW)

# calculate inverse probability weights (IPW) for ATE by assigning weight = 1/PS to treated units and weight = 1/(1-PS) to untreated units

4.3.2.2 Standardized mortality ratio (SMR) treated weights

# calculate weights for ATT by assigning weight = 1 to treated units and weight = PS/(1-PS) to control units

4.3.2.3 Matching weights

# calculate matching weights by assigning weight = min(PS, 1-PS)/PS to treated units and min(PS, 1-PS)/(1-PS) to controls
nsw_cps_plus$opt_weight <- create_matching_weights(nsw_cps_plus, treat, covar)
nsw_psid_plus$opt_weight <- create_matching_weights(nsw_psid_plus, treat, covar)

4.3.2.4 Overlap weights

# calculate overlap weights by assigning weight = 1-PS to treated units and weight = PS, emphasizing units with propensity scores near 0.5
nsw_cps_plus$overlap_weight <- create_overlap_weights(nsw_cps_plus, treat, covar)
nsw_psid_plus$overlap_weight <- create_overlap_weights(nsw_psid_plus, treat, covar)

4.3.2.5 Entropy weights

# calculate entropy weights by assigning weight = -[PS × log(PS) + (1 − PS) × log(1 − PS)]/PS to treated units and -[PS × log(PS) + (1 − PS) × log(1 − PS)]/(1 − PS) to control units

nsw_psid_plus$ebal_weight <- create_entropy_weights(nsw_psid_plus, treat, covar)

4.10 Summary

After reexamining the original LaLonde (NSW) dataset, the results confirm that the NSW-Experimental data shows nearly perfect overlap between treated and control groups, while the observational datasets (NSW-CPS1 and NSW-PSID1) display weak overlap, with many treated units outside the control range. Expanding these datasets with experimental controls improves overlap but does not consistently enhance covariate balance.

These findings reinforce the lessons from previous LDW analyses: even with improved overlap and some gains in covariate balance, achieving consistent and reliable effect estimation remains difficult, especially with non-experimental data. The results highlight the persistent limitations of observational samples in replicating experimental benchmarks.
